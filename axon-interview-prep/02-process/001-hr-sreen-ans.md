# HR Screen Sample Answers

Use simple English. Speak slowly. Do not memorize every word. Remember the main points and speak naturally.

2. Với câu hỏi kể chuyện: STAR-L

Dùng công thức:

S — Situation: Hoàn cảnh
T — Task: Trách nhiệm của mày
A — Action: Mày đã làm gì
R — Result: Kết quả
L — Learning: Mày học được gì

3. Với câu hỏi quan điểm: PREP

Dùng công thức:

P — Point: Quan điểm
R — Reason: Lý do
E — Example: Ví dụ
P — Point: Chốt lại

4. Với câu hỏi về bản thân: Present → Past → Future

Dùng cho “Tell me about yourself”:

Present: Hiện đang làm gì
Past: Trước đây có kinh nghiệm gì liên quan
Future: Đang tìm cơ hội gì

5. Với câu hỏi “Why”: Past value → Current limit → Future fit

Dùng cho:

Why are you leaving?
Why did you change companies?
Why Axon?
Why this role?

Cấu trúc:

Nói điều tích cực đã học được
Nói điều đang tìm kiếm tiếp theo
Liên hệ với công ty mới

## 1. Tell Me About Yourself

> Thank you for having me today. My name is Banh Hao Toan, and you can call me Toan.

> I’m a backend engineer with over two years of experience. Right now, I’m working at Hasaki, mainly on order, payment, and third-party integration systems.

> Before that, I worked as a backend developer at Avian Solutions, and I started my career with a DevOps internship at TechJDI. That experience helped me understand both backend development and how systems run in production.

> Now, I’m looking for a role where I can take on more challenging problems and contribute to products with real impact.

## 2. Why Did You Move From DevOps To Backend?

Recommended answer:

> I started with DevOps because I wanted to understand how real software runs in production.

> That experience was very useful, but over time I realized that I wanted to be closer to product logic and backend system design. I enjoyed working more closely with business logic and building backend features directly.

> I don’t see my DevOps experience as separate from backend development. I see it as using that foundation to become a better backend engineer who understands both application code and production behavior.

Short version:

> I moved from DevOps to backend because I wanted to be closer to product logic and system design. DevOps helped me understand production reliability, and backend lets me build the application behavior directly.

## 3. Why Are You Leaving Your Current Company?

> I’ve learned a lot at Hasaki, especially about production systems. At this stage, I’m looking for a larger product environment with more complex technical challenges. I now want to work in a global product team and take on more complex engineering problems.

Avoid saying:

- The current company is boring.
- The salary is low.
- The teammates are weak.
- You just want any foreign company.

Short version:

## 10. What Are Your Strengths?

Choose 2 or 3 strengths. Do not say all of them in one answer.

### Option A: Ownership

> One of my strengths is ownership. When I work on a feature, I try to understand the problem, not only the task.
>
> For example, in payment and order systems at Hasaki, small bugs can affect many users, so I learned to be careful with correctness and production behavior.

## 11. What Are Your Weaknesses, And How Are You Improving Them?

Choose one. The safest answer is Option A.

### Option A: Learning Slowly At First, But Deeply

> One weakness I am working on is that I sometimes spend too much time trying to understand a new system in detail before starting.

> I like to build a clear understanding, but this can slow me down at the beginning. To improve, I now focus first on the big picture and the information needed for the task. I also ask questions earlier instead of trying to figure out everything by myself.

> This helps me start faster while still learning the system properly over time.

### Option B: Communication

> One weakness I am improving is communication, especially explaining my thinking clearly and sharing context earlier.
> Sometimes I focus too much on solving the technical problem first, so I may not communicate my assumptions or progress early enough.
> I am improving this by giving clearer updates, asking for feedback earlier, and explaining technical topics in a more structured way.
> This has helped me work more smoothly with the team.

## 12. How Do You Use AI In Your Work?

Recommended answer:

> I use AI as an assistant, not as a replacement for my thinking.
>
> I usually use AI to explain unfamiliar topics, suggest edge cases, or compare implementation options.
>
> But I always review the result by myself. For important logic, I still rely on tests, documentation, and my own understanding.

Axon-specific point:

> I think this is important for Axon because AI can help speed up development, but engineers must still be responsible for the final result.

## 13. When Should You Avoid Relying Too Much On AI?

Recommended answer:

> I should avoid relying too much on AI when the work affects security, privacy, production data, or important business logic.
>
> AI can make mistakes or miss project context. So for critical code, I need to understand the logic, test it carefully, and ask for code review.
>
> I also should not put sensitive company data or customer data into AI tools unless the company policy allows it.

## 14. Tell Me About A Project You Are Proud Of
> One project I’m proud of is the payment system I worked on at Hasaki
> I worked on payment flows, transaction statuses, refunds, and third-party integrations. Since payment issues can directly affect customers and business operations, I had to pay close attention to correctness, error handling, and edge cases.
> This project helped me build a stronger production mindset and taught me to think beyond just making the API work.

## 15. Tell Me About A Time You Failed And How You Overcame It

Recommended answer:

> One failure I learned from happened when I worked on a backend task that was more complex than I expected.
>
> At the beginning, I focused too much on implementing the solution by myself. I did not clarify some edge cases early enough, and I underestimated the integration details. Because of that, the first version needed rework after review.
>
> I overcame it by breaking the task into smaller parts, clarifying requirements earlier, and asking for feedback before going too deep into implementation.
>
> The final solution became cleaner. More importantly, I learned to reduce risk earlier instead of trying to solve everything alone.

## 16. Tell Me About A Time You Had Conflict Or Disagreement With A Teammate

Recommended answer:

> In one project, I had a different opinion from a teammate about the technical approach.
>
> I thought one solution was safer because it handled edge cases better, but it also took more time. My teammate preferred a faster solution.
>
> Instead of arguing, I explained my concern and listened to his reason. Then we compared the tradeoffs and chose a solution that was simple enough but still safe.
>
> I learned that disagreement is normal in engineering. The important thing is to discuss with respect and focus on the best result.

> Could I take a few seconds to organize my thoughts?

If you do not understand:

> Sorry, could you please repeat the question?

> Do you mean my technical experience or my motivation for the role?

> I do not have deep experience with that yet. My approach would be to read the documentation, ask good questions, and practice with a small task first.

## 25. How do you usually approach learning a completely new technology or codebase when you need to become productive quickly?
> When I need to learn a new technology or codebase quickly, I start with the big picture: the system’s purpose, architecture, and key business flows.

> I review the documentation and code, and I may use approved AI tools to understand unfamiliar concepts, but I always verify the information. I also ask teammates focused questions about critical flows and common mistakes.

> Finally, I trace one important flow end to end and take on a small real task. This helps me validate my understanding and become productive quickly.

## 26. What would you do if your manager gave you feedback that you strongly disagreed with?
> First, I would make sure I fully understood the feedback and the reasoning behind it. I might ask a few questions because sometimes disagreement comes from having different information or understanding the requirements differently.

> If I still had concerns, I would explain them respectfully and provide concrete examples, such as possible edge cases, technical risks, or effects on the project > timeline. I would focus on the issue rather than trying to prove that my opinion was right.

> I am open to changing my mind when the feedback is reasonable and supported by evidence. If there is an approach that addresses both concerns, I would be happy to discuss it.

> However, I also understand that we may not always reach complete agreement. Once the manager or team has made a final decision, I would support it and implement it professionally, unless I believed it created a serious security, legal, or ethical risk that needed to be escalated.

## 29. How do you handle stress or pressure at work?
> When I feel stressed, I usually take a short break to clear my mind first.

> After that, I break the work into smaller tasks and start with the simpler ones. Finishing a few small tasks helps me regain focus and confident. Then I move on to the more difficult or important tasks with a clearer mind.

## 30. How do you handle a production incident?
> When I find a production incident, I first report it clearly to the team and share the impact, possible cause, and current status.
> Then I suggest a temporary fix to reduce the impact as quickly as possible. If needed, I also propose a long-term solution to prevent the same issue from happening again.
> After that, I wait for the team or manager to approve the approach. While waiting, I can prepare the code changes or draft the implementation, but I would not deploy anything without approval.

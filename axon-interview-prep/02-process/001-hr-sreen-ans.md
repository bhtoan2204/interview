# HR Screen Sample Answers

Use simple English. Speak slowly. Do not memorize every word. Remember the main points and speak naturally.

>
> I graduated about two years ago with a Bachelor's degree in Software Engineering. My main focus is backend development.

> I am a backend developer with experience in Go, TypeScript, payment systems, high-traffic order systems, cloud infrastructure, and observability. I enjoy building reliable backend services and learning new technical areas.

## 2. Why Did You Move From DevOps To Backend?

Recommended answer:

> I started with DevOps because I wanted to understand how real software runs in production: deployment, cloud infrastructure, monitoring, logs, and system reliability.
>
> That experience was very useful, but over time I realized that I wanted to be closer to product logic and backend system design. I enjoy building APIs, designing data models, handling business rules, and thinking about reliability from inside the application, not only from the infrastructure side.
>
> Moving to backend felt natural for me because my DevOps background still helps a lot. When I build backend services, I also think about deployment, observability, scalability, failure cases, and how to debug production issues.
>
> So I do not see it as leaving DevOps completely. I see it as using that foundation to become a better backend engineer who understands both application code and production operations.

Short version:

> I moved from DevOps to backend because I wanted to be closer to product logic and system design. DevOps helped me understand deployment, monitoring, and production reliability. Backend lets me build the application logic directly, while still using my DevOps mindset to think about observability, scalability, and production issues.

## 7. What Are Your Strengths?

Choose 2 or 3 strengths. Do not say all of them in one answer.

### Option A: Ownership

> One of my strengths is ownership. When I work on a feature, I try to understand the problem, not only the task. I care about API design, edge cases, testing, logs, and production behavior.
>
> For example, in payment and order systems at Hasaki, small bugs can affect many users, so I learned to be careful with retries, idempotency, state changes, and monitoring.

### Option B: Learning Fast

> Another strength is that I can learn new technical areas quickly. I started with DevOps, then moved more deeply into backend development. I have worked with Go, TypeScript, Kafka, Redis, Debezium CDC, OpenTelemetry, Docker, Kubernetes, and AWS.

> For example, I think about timeouts, retries, duplicate requests, logs, metrics, and how to debug the issue later. This is important for backend systems and also very important for Axon's mission-critical software.

### Short Answer

> My main strengths are ownership, learning fast, and reliability mindset. I care about building backend services that work well in production, not only in local testing.

## 8. What Are Your Weaknesses, And How Are You Improving Them?

Choose one. The safest answer is Option A.

### Option A: Learning Slowly At First, But Deeply

> One weakness I am improving is that I can be slow at the beginning when learning a new system or a new technical area.
>
> The reason is that I like to understand things deeply, not only at the surface level. For example, when I learn a new system, I want to understand the architecture, the data flow, the failure cases, and why the team made certain decisions. This helps me build a strong foundation, but at the beginning it can take more time.
>
> I am improving this by balancing depth with speed. First, I try to understand the big picture and the most important parts needed for the task. Then I ask questions earlier, take notes, and go deeper step by step. This helps me contribute sooner while still building deep understanding over time.
>
> I think this working style can become a strength when managed well, because once I understand something deeply, I can work more independently and make better technical decisions.

Short version:

> One weakness I am improving is that I can be slow at the beginning when learning a new system. I like to understand things deeply, including the architecture, data flow, and failure cases. I am improving by learning the big picture first, asking questions earlier, and going deeper step by step, so I can contribute sooner while still building strong understanding.

### Option B: Communication

> One weakness I am improving is communication, especially explaining my thinking clearly and sharing context earlier.
>
> As a backend developer, sometimes I focus deeply on solving the technical problem first, and I may not communicate my assumptions, tradeoffs, or progress early enough. I realized that good engineering is not only about writing correct code, but also about helping the team understand the approach and align sooner.
>
> I am improving this by writing clearer updates, asking for feedback earlier, and practicing how to explain technical topics in simple English. For example, before implementing a complex change, I try to summarize the problem, the options, and the tradeoffs so teammates can review my direction sooner.
>
> I think this is a good weakness for me to work on because better communication makes code reviews, debugging, and collaboration much more effective.

Short version:

> One weakness I am improving is communication. Sometimes I focus too much on solving the technical problem first, so I may not share my assumptions or progress early enough. I am improving by giving clearer updates, asking for feedback earlier, and practicing how to explain technical topics in simple English.

### Option C: Asking For Feedback Too Late

> One weakness is that sometimes I try to solve a problem by myself for too long before asking for feedback.
>
> I am improving this by sharing my approach earlier, especially when the task has many tradeoffs. This helps me avoid wasting time and also helps the team align sooner.

### Option D: Wanting The First Version To Be Too Perfect

> Sometimes I spend too much time trying to make the first version too perfect.
>
> I am improving this by splitting work into smaller steps, getting feedback earlier, and focusing on the most important requirement first. After that, I can improve the solution step by step.

## 9. How Do You Use AI In Your Work?

Recommended answer:

> I use AI as an assistant, not as a replacement for my thinking.
>
> I usually use AI to explain unfamiliar topics, suggest edge cases, draft simple boilerplate, review possible test cases, or compare different implementation options.
>
> But I always review the result by myself. I check if the code is correct, secure, readable, and follows the project style. For important logic, I still rely on tests, documentation, code review, and my own understanding.

Axon-specific point:

> I think this is important for Axon because the company works in an AI-first engineering environment, but the software is still mission-critical. AI can help speed up development, but engineers must still be responsible for the final result.

## 10. When Should You Avoid Relying Too Much On AI?

Recommended answer:

> I should avoid relying too much on AI when the work affects security, privacy, payments, production data, or important business logic.
>
> AI can make mistakes or miss project context. So for critical code, I need to understand the logic, write tests, check edge cases, and ask for code review.
>
> I also should not put sensitive company data or customer data into AI tools unless the company policy allows it.

## 12. Tell Me About A Time You Failed And How You Overcame It

Recommended answer:

> One failure I learned from happened when I worked on a backend task that was more complex than I first expected.
>
> At the beginning, I focused too much on implementing the solution by myself. I did not clarify some edge cases early enough, and I also underestimated the integration details. Because of that, the first version needed rework after review.
>
> After that, I changed my approach. I broke the task into smaller parts, clarified the requirements earlier, wrote down the main assumptions, and asked for feedback before going too deep into implementation. I also added more tests and logs around the risky parts so the behavior would be easier to verify and debug.
>
> The result was positive. The final solution became cleaner and safer, and I delivered it with a better understanding of the system. More importantly, I learned that when a task is complex, I should not only work harder. I should communicate earlier, validate assumptions, and reduce risk step by step.

Short version:

> One failure I learned from was underestimating a backend task and not clarifying edge cases early enough. The first version needed rework. I overcame it by breaking the task into smaller parts, asking for feedback earlier, documenting assumptions, and adding better tests and logs. The final result was better, and I learned to reduce risk earlier instead of trying to solve everything alone.
